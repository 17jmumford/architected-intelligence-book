# Book Summary
Authored by Jacob Miller and Jeremy Mumford, Architected Intelligence is a comprehensive guide focused on designing and building AI systems and teams with a holistic perspective. The text aims to educate business and technical leaders on how to successfully lead teams and organizations in building AI-first technologies and cultures.
The key framework that ties the book together is the Architected Intelligence framework. This model is designed to be versatile yet complete, serving as a dual-purpose blueprint for both strategy and implementation. On a macro level, the framework provides the structure necessary for orchestrating an organizational transformation to become AI-first. Simultaneously, it scales down to the technical level, framing the specifications required to engineer a single, specific piece of AI technology.


By walking through the framework, we can rapidly summarize the content of the entire book, speedrunning through the core ideas (while skipping the many helpful case studies, key principles, and various tips and tricks).
## Component 1: Output
Output represents the starting point premised on beginning with the end in mind and applying AI at the highest leverage points of both individual projects and organization structures. Chapter one begins by recalibrating the readers' paradigm to build unified systems, rather than distinct “AI systems” separated from “human systems.” When designing these user-agnostic systems, the reader should interrogate why they want to introduce AI. Identify your competitive edge, and identify where AI can strengthen it. 
This foundation leads into the second chapter, where readers are guided through a framework for gathering a broad spectrum of potential AI applications. These opportunities are then evaluated and prioritized based on aspects such as strategic relevance, quality vs impact, frequency vs value, and ensuring feasibility to avoid “failing at the 98% mark,” where projects collapse just prior to completion due to unforeseen complexities.
Once you’ve mastered the design of user-agnostic systems and projects with sufficient strategic relevance, impact, and feasibility you’re ready for Chapter 3: Building battle-tested AI agents and workflows. We introduce the four I’s framework for designing agentic workflows: Initiate, Inspect, Improve, and Implement. This component concludes with a deep dive into the architecture of agents, equipping readers with the understanding needed to navigate their tradeoffs: imperative vs declarative design, tool curation, human integration, goal decomposition, sub-agents, and more.

## Component 2: Input

With clear objectives established and the architectural blueprint for your AI systems in place, you must provide the AI systems with the necessary fuel to execute its mission. Without relevant and valuable input data, your AI systems will likely either a) fail to deliver, or b) become commoditized, offering no distinctive value over standard off-the-shelf models. The framework that unifies this component together is the Knowledge Transformation Cycle, which integrates the platforms and the people involved in the journey of information as it is continuously updated and improved. 
Chapter 4 posits that AI is unlocking a new era of data potential, allowing organizations to leverage data at a breadth and scale previously considered impossible. Organizations must focus on identifying data opportunities, prioritizing them, and then acquiring said data. The intricacies of how this happens, why it’s important, and where the data can be sourced are explored.
Once you’ve collected valuable data, chapter 5 focuses on the “logistics” of serving it. This is the “last mile” challenge, ensuring that the right data reaches the right people in the right form at the right moment. We cover advanced search strategies, including Retrieval Augmented Generation, hybrid search, and multi-stage strategies. Readers are then presented with the radical reconceptualization: we propose viewing the humans involved in the process as high-value, high-cost nodes in a network. In this model, AI systems can bidirectionally interact with these people (nodes), requesting complex knowledge or handing off ambiguous tasks. Various connection patterns are also covered, including conversational interfaces, copilots, and transactional systems. 
Chapter 6 represents one of the most ambitious sections of the book, proposing a comprehensive model for curating and managing knowledge bases in an organization. We introduce a “knowledge pyramid” where information is categorized and through curation motions, information is gathered and refined as it flows upward until it is considered “canon,” or information that can be confidently communicated to an AI. The concepts in this chapter are complex but powerful, allowing organizations to elevate their knowledge bases from static repositories of outdated junk to dynamic rocket fuel necessary to propel AI systems to the next level of performance.
## Component 3: Model

At component 3, we finally arrive at the engine powering our AI-first organization: the AI model itself. However, the engine is limited by its fuel intake system, and data must be processed and injected into our model to maximize its efficacy. 
Chapter 7 introduces readers to “context engineering,” carefully taking data from both structured (numerical) and unstructured (text, audio, image, video), and using prompt engineering to weave it together. While teams can use that same data for fine tuning, we caution against premature optimization. We advocate for a hierarchy of interventions before taking on the operational and financial burden of fine tuning, leaning on context engineering, caching, and memory to boost model performance.
Once readers possess a thorough grasp of increasing model performance through data, we turn to the architectural decisions required to consistently select and optimize models. We analyze the triangle of AI model tradeoffs of cost, latency, and intelligence, forming one of the central frameworks. We also navigate the complex landscape of AI providers, contrasting the offerings of foundation model labs, specialized inference providers, hyperscalers, and AI routers. Finally, we review advanced techniques such as self-hosting, compression, dynamic routing, and multi-model orchestration to help squeeze the last ounce of performance.
## Component 4: Observability
Generative AI created a collision between the distinct disciplines of software engineering and data science. The authors themselves represent this cross-pollination, and we observe that many practitioners operating in this nascent space possess deep expertise in one domain while remaining novices in the other. Software engineers typically excel at system observability, but struggle to benchmark and evaluate their AI systems. Conversely, data scientists are adept at statistical evaluation and model controls, but may lack the tooling to monitor production reliability. Component 4 aims to bridge this gap, covering the essentials of observability in both disciplines while mapping their evolution in the era of generative AI.
Chapter 8 focuses on understanding the WHAT of your AI systems using traditional observability platforms. We utilize the AWS observability maturity model as a structural backbone for the chapter, teaching readers both the basics of software observability while also illustrating to veterans how their traditional metrics must adapt to the non-deterministic nature of generative AI systems.
Chapter 9 pivots on understanding the WHY of your AI systems, teaching readers how to evaluate and benchmark their AI agents and workflows. The key insight is that while “trust” is essential, it is too abstract to measure directly. Trust must be disaggregated into its underlying components. The framework introduced here is TACA: Transparency, Accuracy, Calibration, and Alignment. We explore various methods for identifying and satisfying TACA requirements by leveraging evaluation datasets and applying them throughout the AI system, and not just focusing on final outputs.
## Component 5: Enablement
Enablement serves the bedrock consisting of both the people and platforms necessary to make your AI-first transformation scalable and sustainable.
Chapter 11 is about people, the most essential element of an AI-first organization. Leaders have a responsibility to lead the charge on AI, equipping their team members the AI tools to succeed in this new era. Readers learn how to identify the DNA of AI-ready individuals and how to cultivate the internal champions who will lead teams to victory. When considering talent acquisition and deployment, we critique both the centralized “ivory tower” approach and the decentralized “wild west” approach. Instead, we advocate a “hub and spoke” approach to balance the distribution of AI resources and localized expertise.
Chapter 12 shifts focus to platforms. Throughout the text, we have alluded to various AI platforms. The first half of the chapter codifies the core principles that define a successful AI platform: modularity, scalability, results-orientation, broad access, and progressive quality control. The latter half of the chapter aggregates the various AI systems discussed throughout the book and provides an explainer for each. These include coordination platforms, support platforms, and action platforms, clarifying how these distinct layers integrate to form a technology stack.
